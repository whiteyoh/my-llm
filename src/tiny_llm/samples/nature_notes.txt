In the school garden, students observed three butterflies near the lavender plants.
A robin gathered twigs for a nest while bees visited yellow flowers.
After light rain, the soil smelled fresh and tiny worms appeared on the path.
The class recorded clouds, wind, and leaf colors in a science notebook.

Morning note: The maple leaves were green at the center and gold at the edges.
Two snails moved slowly under the bench where the ground stayed cool.
The teacher asked everyone to draw before naming, so the shapes came first.
Several students noticed that the same plant looked different in shade and sun.

Midday note: The pond reflected white clouds and a line of tall reeds.
Water striders crossed the surface in quick, careful steps.
A dragonfly rested on a warm stone, then lifted away like a blue needle.
The class measured the air temperature and wrote it beside the sketch.

Afternoon note: Wind moved through the grass in soft waves.
The seed heads bent, rose, and bent again.
Students compared the garden sounds: leaves rubbing, birds calling, pencils writing.
They learned that quiet observation can still be full of detail.

End note: The garden changed during one school day.
Light shifted, insects moved, and damp soil dried at the top.
The notebook did not contain every answer, but it held careful evidence.
Tomorrow the class will return and see what changed again.
